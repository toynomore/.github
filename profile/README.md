# Toy No More

Every embedded engineer has seen them: the endless stream of "toy projects" scattered across tutorials and maker blogs.

Blink an LED.  
Read a sensor.  
Move a servo.

Most people treat them as disposable exercises, things you try once and forget. But what happens if we stop dismissing them as toys and take them seriously?

Toy No More is a hackathon that flips the narrative: instead of building one tutorial project, we build **all of them**.


## Why

Even in courses like DTEP or DTAP, we often end up focusing on a very small subset of what embedded systems can do. Those courses are great for learning core concepts, but the space of possible embedded applications is far larger than what fits into a single course project.

Toy No More is an opportunity to expand that vocabulary. The goal is to explore many small patterns, devices, and interactions that normally remain outside the curriculum.


## The Idea

Over the course of a weekend, we will turn the place into a miniature embedded systems factory.

Participants will pick from a growing list of classic microcontroller projects, such as:

- LED animations and traffic lights  
- sensor monitors (temperature, motion, distance)  
- motor and servo controllers  
- small robots and line-following cars  
- display dashboards and digital clocks  
- wireless sensor nodes and IoT devices  
- audio and signal processing experiments  
- simple games and interactive gadgets

Teams will systematically implement them until the internet's catalogue of "toy projects" has been exhausted.

Each completed build gets documented and demoed.

The rule is simple:

**If it exists as a tutorial, we will make it real.**


## Have Fun, and Leave a Mark

Each participant **becomes a contributor** to a collective public repository documenting all the projects built during the event.

The repository will contain:

- source code
- wiring diagrams
- documentation
- short engineering notes

This can serve as a portfolio reference and a shared catalogue of embedded experiments.


## The Twist

Building is only half the story.

Each team will also present **one deep engineering problem** they encountered during implementation. It should be something you only notice after actually trying to build the system.

Examples might include:

- timing issues
- noisy sensor data
- unstable power supply
- communication latency
- tricky software bugs

These short walkthroughs will turn the weekend into a shared learning experience and give participants concrete engineering stories that often come up in technical interviews.


## What Happens When You Build Many Small Systems

As the weekend progresses, something interesting happens.

The projects stop feeling like toys.

Participants begin to see the patterns that connect them. They see how sensors feed control loops, how signals get processed, how devices communicate, and how systems fail when power, timing, or wiring goes wrong.

Individually the builds are small. Together they form a living map of embedded systems design.


## The Result

By the end of the weekend, the room will be filled with blinking lights, buzzing alarms, moving motors, dashboards, robots, and experimental devices.

It will be a chaotic but fascinating snapshot of embedded systems in action.

To an outsider, many of these projects may still look like toys. But for the people who built them, each one represents a set of real engineering challenges solved, lessons learned, and systems understood.

Come build, debug, and explore.

Bring curiosity, patience, and a willingness to wire one more circuit.

By the end of the weekend, these projects may still look simple, but they will not feel like toys anymore. And who knows, some of them may even grow into something bigger after the event.
